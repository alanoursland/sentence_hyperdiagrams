# The Cambridge Grammar of the English Language (CGEL)

**Authors:** Rodney Huddleston, Geoffrey K. Pullum (+ 13 contributors)
**Published:** Cambridge University Press, 2002
**ISBN:** 978-0-521-43146-0
**Pages:** 1,842

## What it is

The definitive modern descriptive grammar of English. Replaces Quirk et al.
(1985) as the standard reference. Describes English as it is actually used,
not as prescriptivists say it should be.

### Coverage

- Verb and noun phrase structure
- Clause types and complementation
- Coordination and subordination
- Relative clauses
- Tense, aspect, mood, modality
- Negation
- Information structure (topic, focus)
- Deixis
- Word formation (morphology)

### Key features

- **Descriptive, not prescriptive** — documents actual usage patterns
- **Comprehensive** — 1,842 pages covering essentially every English
  construction
- **Modernized terminology** — uses clearer, more consistent category names
  than traditional grammar

## Relevance

When annotating early reader text and encountering unusual, colloquial, or
archaic constructions (the dataset is from 1880s-1910s), CGEL provides the
authoritative structural analysis. Ensures the hyperdiagram ontology handles
real English, not just textbook examples:

- Resolves ambiguous syntactic structures
- Clarifies the internal structure of complex NPs, VPs, and clauses
- Provides the linguistic foundation that UD dependency labels formalize
